# Luna Placement Debate

Where should the 13th month "Luna" be placed in the GHS calendar?

## Model A: Luna as Month 5 (Summer Position)

Luna sits between June and July, creating a long summer block of four months.

**Pros:**
- Creates a dedicated "Light Month" in the heart of summer
- Psychologically extends the warm season — a global holiday month
- Follows the IFC tradition: Moses Cotsworth placed his extra month "Sol" in midsummer for exactly this reason
- Cultural appeal: summer vacation gets its own month

**Cons:**
- September becomes the 8th month, not the 7th — the Latin etymology ("septem" = 7) remains broken, just as it is in the Gregorian calendar
- Similarly, October (8th instead of "octo" = 8), November (9th instead of "novem" = 9), and December (10th instead of "decem" = 10) remain misnamed

## Model B: Luna as Month 13 (End of Year)

Luna is placed after February, right before the new year begins in March.

**Pros:**
- **Etymological healing**: With March as month 1 and no extra month inserted before September, the Latin month names finally align:
  - September = month 7 (septem = 7)
  - October = month 8 (octo = 8)
  - November = month 9 (novem = 9)
  - December = month 10 (decem = 10)
- Luna (the Moon) as the final month fits symbolically — a time of darkness, reflection, and preparation before the spring equinox
- The Aurora Week follows Luna as a standalone period (A.1–A.7), not part of any month — a clean separation between the old year and the new

**Cons:**
- The extra month falls in late winter (February–March Gregorian), not in the sunny season
- No dedicated summer holiday month
- Less cultural excitement

## The Decision

**Model B was chosen.** Luna is the 13th and final month.

The reasoning: a calendar that claims to "heal the history of time" cannot leave the most glaring etymological wound untreated. For over 2,000 years, September has not been the 7th month. GHS finally restores this — and October (8), November (9), and December (10) with it.

Luna as the Moon Month at year's end carries deep symbolism: darkness before dawn, reflection before renewal. The Aurora Week (A.1–A.7) then follows as a standalone interlude — time outside of time — before March brings the spring equinox and a new year.

Model A (summer position) was considered for its cultural appeal, but mathematical and historical integrity won out. A calendar for a species that values truth — even when the sunny option is tempting.
