# Historical Context

Why does our calendar look the way it does — and why does it need to change?

## The Broken Inheritance

Our current timekeeping is a patchwork of ancient compromises, imperial vanity, and colonial power:

### The Roman Legacy (Months)

The Roman calendar originally had 10 months, starting in March. January and February were added later by King Numa Pompilius (~700 BCE). When Julius Caesar reformed the calendar in 46 BCE, he kept the month names — but the numbering was now wrong:

- **September** (septem = 7) became the 9th month
- **October** (octo = 8) became the 10th month
- **November** (novem = 9) became the 11th month
- **December** (decem = 10) became the 12th month

Later, **July** (Julius Caesar) and **August** (Augustus Caesar) were renamed from Quintilis and Sextilis — emperors inserting their names into the calendar itself. Month lengths were adjusted to flatter imperial egos (Augustus demanded 31 days like Julius).

### The Papal Correction (Leap Year)

In 1582, Pope Gregory XIII introduced the Gregorian calendar to fix the drift of the Julian calendar. His correction (skip 3 leap years every 400 years) was mathematically sound but politically fraught — Protestant and Orthodox nations refused to adopt it for centuries. Greece didn't switch until 1923. The "universal" calendar was really a Catholic one for hundreds of years.

### The Colonial Meridian (Time Zones)

At the International Meridian Conference in 1884, the Western industrial powers — led by Britain, then the world's dominant colonial and naval force — voted to place the prime meridian at Greenwich, London.

- **Why Greenwich?** Not geography but commerce: 72% of the world's maritime trade already used British charts referencing Greenwich. It was a decision of economic dominance.
- **French resistance**: France refused to acknowledge Greenwich for decades, using the Paris meridian until 1911.
- **The International Date Line**: Pushed into the Pacific to avoid splitting any major Western nation — a convenience for the powerful at the expense of Pacific island nations, which still deal with bizarre time zone anomalies today.

## The Egyptian 24 Hours

The Egyptians divided the day using a base-12 system. By counting the three joints on each of four fingers (thumb excluded), they arrived at 12. Ten daylight hours plus two twilight transition hours made 12 daytime hours. At night, they tracked 12 "decan" star constellations rising above the horizon.

12 + 12 = 24 hours.

Originally, these were **seasonal hours** — longer in summer, shorter in winter. Fixed-length hours only emerged with the spread of mechanical clocks in medieval Europe.

## The Babylonian 60

The Sumerians and Babylonians (~4,000 years ago) used base-60 (sexagesimal) because 60 has extraordinarily many divisors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30. In a world without calculators, this made mental division practical.

The 360° circle, the 60-minute hour, and the 60-second minute are all inherited from this system. Our current time structure — 24 × 60 × 60 = 86,400 seconds per day — is a 4,000-year-old compromise between Egyptian astronomy and Babylonian arithmetic.
